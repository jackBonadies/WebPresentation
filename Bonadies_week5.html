Open Source vs Proprietary Software for business


by Jack Bonadies

Why businesses should use Open Source software

  • Linus' Law prevents bugs and vulnerabilities
    • The reading mentions a small change to OpenSSL (Open Source) introducing a vulnerability that lasted for 2.5 years. Despite this, proprietary OS's and SW (Windows, Mac, iOS, etc.) all have hosts of vulnerabilities which can go undetected or worse detected and unpatched for years.
  • Proximity of business to the open source community prompts sponsorship, increased eyes, increased contributors, and increased adoption
    • Though, corporate donations are tiny in comparison to profits make off the backs of the labor of open source developers. Also, it can be hard to distribute donations in a way thats in line with community beliefs and values.
    • According to the article, most open source developments come from tech employees. A counterargument, is most of the profits made from Open Source, go back to tech companies.
  • Cuts costs of redundant labor
    • Companies should use open source resources rather than "reinvent the wheel" on duplicate development efforts. This wastes the labor power of society.
  • The "Network Externalities Assumption" that networks / standards / languages get more valuable the more nodes / conformances / speakers they include
    • This is very apparent with the value of the world wide web. I could create my own set of standards for distributing / sharing information that may be technically "better" but if no one uses them other than me, they are worthless.
  • It is far easier to modify or learn from because the source code is available. In this way, the Software can be altered for your specific needs and shared.
  • If the open source software that one uses is licensed under the GPL or other copyleft licenses, this will ensure that the company must release their SW under the same license. This means that employees working for the company will know that their efforts will benefit the whole of society's knowledge, and that they are not squandering their own labor power.
    • Furthermore, a lot of employees will feel uncomfortable working on and therefore supporting the contradictory concept of "intellectual property". Many believe that it fetters technological development, wastes social labor power, contradictorily tries to make exclusive immaterial ideas - duplicated at virtually no cost, is antisocial (excluding others, keeping info from them), and producing something many see as negative and exploitative as intellectual property is a form of capital.

Why businesses may not want to use open source software

  • Copyleft licenses such as the GPL (used by Linux, Apache, MySQL) may prevent the business from selling their product if it includes GPL licensed components
  • Guilt / Moral Reasons.
    • If a company commodifies and profits from the intellect of others, while declaring their own contributions to be their "intellectual property" this could create feelings of guilt or cognitive dissonance.
    • They would be using software that benefits all of society in order to make software that purposefully excludes those who can't pay as well as hiding their code so that others cannot learn from or use it.
    • Many believe that all knowledge claimed as intellectual property has been taken from the community.
  • There may not be tech support or maintenance for open source software
    • Some companies such as RedHat do try to bridge this gap however, by providing paid services for open source software.

Who contributes to Open Source projects?

The article mentioned that a significant number of git contributors are from for profit corporations. However, they did not give any data. I was able to find data online (source at bottom of page and last slide) showing the number of github contributors associated with the top 30 companies. Note that every github contributor may not display their company so the data may not be entirely accurate.

However, the data does give the total number of contributors to be: 2,060,011. Hence, to see percentage of total rather than raw number alternatively .
